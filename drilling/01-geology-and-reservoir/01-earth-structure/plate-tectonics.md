## Overview

Plate tectonics is the scientific framework that explains how the outer shell of the Earth is broken into large rigid pieces, called plates, that move slowly across the planet's surface. Plate motion reshapes continents over millions of years, builds mountain belts, opens oceans, and pulls continental crust (the solid rocky outermost layer of the Earth) apart into deep basins. For a driller (the worker who operates the drilling controls) on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf), plate tectonics is the root cause of everything that makes the North Sea and the Norwegian Sea worth drilling in the first place. The basins that contain oil and gas on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) were formed by plate-tectonic stretching of continental crust (the solid outer rocky layer of the Earth), not by chance (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

Understanding plate tectonics gives a driller (the worker who operates the drilling controls) a mental picture of why formations appear in the order they do, why pressure regimes change with depth, and why structures such as the Viking Graben (a deep, fault-bounded trough in the northern North Sea) hold the hydrocarbons (oil and gas, the target of drilling) they hold. This article is written for a reader with no prior geology background. It introduces plates, their boundaries, and the forces that move them, then connects those concepts to the geological history of the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) so that the reader can reason from plate tectonics to why a specific NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) well is drilled in a particular place to a particular depth.

## Details

### The Earth's outer layers

The Earth is not uniform from surface to centre. Four main zones matter when setting up plate tectonics: the crust (the solid rocky outer layer), the mantle (the much thicker layer of hot rock below the crust), the outer core (liquid iron and nickel deeper still), and the inner core (solid iron and nickel at the centre). Drilling only ever touches the crust. On the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf), even the deepest wells reach just a few kilometres into the crust, which itself is typically between 25 and 35 kilometres thick under the Norwegian mainland and between 15 and 25 kilometres thick under much of the North Sea.

Two concepts matter most for plate tectonics. The first is the lithosphere (the rigid outer shell of the Earth, made of the crust plus the top of the mantle, behaving as one brittle plate that can break along faults). The second is the asthenosphere (the hotter, slowly flowing layer of mantle rock just beneath the lithosphere, on which the lithosphere slides). The lithosphere (the rigid shell of crust plus upper mantle) is typically about 100 kilometres thick under oceans and 150 to 250 kilometres thick under continents. The asthenosphere (the ductile layer below the rigid shell) is not liquid; it is still solid rock, but it is hot enough to flow slowly, similar in behaviour to very thick toffee. The distinction is mechanical, not chemical: a plate is a piece of lithosphere (the rigid outer shell) that is strong enough to move as one, while the asthenosphere (the ductile mantle layer below) is weak enough to flow and therefore lets plates slide.

### Plates and how they move

A plate is a single slab of lithosphere (the rigid shell of crust plus upper mantle). There are seven large plates, including the Eurasian Plate on which Norway sits, and many smaller ones. Plates move relative to each other at speeds of a few centimetres per year, roughly the speed a fingernail grows. This motion is imperceptible on a human timescale, but over tens of millions of years it opens oceans and builds mountain chains.

Plates meet at plate boundaries (the edges where two plates are in contact with each other). Plate boundaries come in three kinds, and each kind produces a different geology and therefore a different set of drilling problems and opportunities:

- Divergent boundaries, where two plates pull apart and new crust (new solid rocky outer layer) forms in the gap. Mid-ocean ridges (underwater mountain chains where two plates spread and new oceanic crust is created) are the clearest example. Divergent motion also creates rifts (zones where continental crust is being pulled apart and thinned), including the rift that opened the North Atlantic and stretched the crust beneath today's North Sea.

- Convergent boundaries, where two plates move toward each other. If one plate is oceanic and one is continental, the oceanic plate usually sinks beneath the continental plate in a process called subduction (one plate descending beneath another back into the mantle). Convergent margins build mountain belts such as the Himalayas and volcanic arcs such as Japan. The NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) has no active convergent boundary today, but the Scandinavian mountain chain (the Caledonides) is the eroded remnant of a plate collision that ended more than 400 million years ago, and the deep roots of that old collision sit beneath parts of the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

- Transform boundaries, where two plates slide past each other horizontally without creating or destroying crust (the rocky outer layer). The San Andreas Fault in California is the textbook example. The NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) does not sit on a transform boundary today either, but transform offsets shape the edges of North Atlantic spreading, and features such as the Jan Mayen Fracture Zone west of Norway are transform structures.

### Forces driving plate motion

Plates do not slide on their own. They are driven by a set of forces that all trace back to heat inside the Earth:

- Mantle convection (the slow circulation of hot rock in the mantle, where hot material rises and cooler material sinks), which drags the base of the lithosphere (the rigid outer shell) sideways.
- Ridge push (the force from elevated mid-ocean ridges, which stand higher than the surrounding seafloor and push plates gravitationally away from the ridge axis).
- Slab pull (the force from a sinking oceanic plate at a subduction zone, which pulls the rest of the plate behind it as it descends). Slab pull is generally considered the strongest of the three forces driving modern plates.

For the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf), the force that mattered most historically was none of these three as they operate today. It was the extensional stretching that thinned continental crust (the solid outermost rocky layer of the Earth) during Permian (the geological period from about 299 to 252 million years ago), Triassic (from about 252 to 200 million years ago), and Jurassic (from about 200 to 145 million years ago) rifting. That stretching lowered the crust, created deep basins, and set up the conditions for source rocks (organic-rich rocks that generate oil and gas when buried and heated), reservoir rocks (porous rocks that store oil and gas in the pore spaces between grains), and seals (rocks that prevent hydrocarbons from escaping upwards) to form in the right vertical order.

### Plate tectonics and petroleum systems

Hydrocarbons (oil and gas, the target of drilling) are found almost exclusively in sedimentary basins (large depressions in the Earth's crust filled with layered sediments). Sedimentary basins (these sediment-filled depressions in the crust) are formed mainly by plate-tectonic processes. Three of the most important basin types on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) are tectonic in origin:

- Rift basins, formed where continental crust (the rocky outer layer of a continent) has been stretched, faulted, and dropped down along parallel normal faults (fractures where the two sides have moved apart, with one side dropping). The Viking Graben and Central Graben in the North Sea are textbook rift basins.
- Passive-margin basins, formed on a continental edge after active rifting stops and the margin subsides slowly under its own sediment load. The mid-Norwegian margin, including the Vøring and Møre Basins, is a passive-margin basin today.
- Sag basins, formed by broad thermal subsidence after rifting, without a sharply faulted boundary.

Plate tectonics therefore controls where source rocks (organic-rich rocks that generate hydrocarbons), reservoir rocks (porous rocks that hold hydrocarbons), and seals (rocks that trap hydrocarbons) are deposited, how deeply they are later buried (which governs whether a source rock matures into oil or gas), and whether structural traps (underground geometries that capture migrating hydrocarbons) such as tilted fault-bounded blocks exist (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

## NCS-specific context

The NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) today is a passive margin (a continental edge with no active plate boundary, tectonically quiet), but its present geometry is the result of a long sequence of plate-tectonic events (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview). The relevant history, in compressed form:

1. Caledonian collision, roughly 440 to 400 million years ago. An ocean called Iapetus closed between the ancient Laurentia continental block (future North America plus Greenland) and the Baltica continental block (future Scandinavia). The collision welded them and built a mountain chain comparable in scale to the Himalayas. The eroded roots of that chain underlie much of mainland Norway and extend beneath part of the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

2. Permian (the geological period from about 299 to 252 million years ago) to early Triassic (about 252 to 250 million years ago) rifting. The combined Laurentia-plus-Baltica continental block began to pull apart. Early rifts produced thick evaporite (salt) deposits in the southern North Sea; those salt layers remain today as the mobile Zechstein Salt (a thick Permian-age salt unit that flows under overburden load and deforms overlying rock) and control trap geometries in the Central Graben (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

3. Jurassic (from about 200 to 145 million years ago) rifting. The Viking Graben and Central Graben deepened. Source rocks (organic-rich rocks that generate oil and gas when buried and heated) including the Upper Jurassic Draupne Formation, the dominant source rock for most NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) oil fields, were deposited in the restricted rift basins. Reservoir sandstones (porous sandstones that store oil and gas) including the Brent Group (Middle Jurassic) were laid down on the rift shoulders and in the rift interior. This is the single most productive interval on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Fields and discoveries).

4. Late Cretaceous (from about 100 to 66 million years ago) to Paleocene (from about 66 to 56 million years ago) thermal sag and final continental breakup. Seafloor spreading jumped to the line between Greenland and Norway, and true oceanic crust (new basaltic crust formed at a spreading ridge) began forming in what is now the Norwegian Sea around 55 million years ago (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

5. Cenozoic (the geological era from about 66 million years ago to today) subsidence and burial, with progressive deposition of thick Paleocene (about 66 to 56 million years ago) and younger sands (including the Utsira and Heimdal reservoirs) and overpressured shales that now present pore-pressure challenges during drilling (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

6. Pleistocene (the geological epoch from about 2.6 million years ago to 11,700 years ago) glacial erosion and isostatic rebound (the slow upward rise of crust after an ice load is removed). Repeated ice sheets carved the Norwegian Trench, loaded the crust, and then unloaded it. Isostatic rebound (the ongoing rise of the crust after ice retreat) continues today.

For a driller (the worker who operates the drilling controls) on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf), the operational consequences of this plate-tectonic history are concrete:

- Basin architecture determines casing design. A well drilled into the Central Graben passes through post-rift sag deposits, mobile Zechstein Salt (the Permian salt unit that flows under load), and rift-fill sediments of sharply changing pressure. Each of these has its own implications for mud weight and casing setting depth (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).
- Rift-era faults create structural traps and also create shallow-gas hazards. The same faults that trap hydrocarbons (oil and gas) can also transmit shallow gas toward the surface, which is why shallow-gas planning is a standard part of NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) well design (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).
- Paleocene (about 66 to 56 million years ago) sand bodies buried by later Cenozoic (the last 66 million years) shales are often overpressured. The driller (the worker operating the drilling controls) and the boreleder (drilling supervisor) need to plan mud weight for pore-pressure ramps in those intervals.
- The Caledonian basement (the old mountain-root rocks from the 440 to 400 million-year-old collision) forms the deep floor of several NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) basins and defines the lower limit of drillable prospect space for most plays (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Geology overview).

Plate-tectonic understanding is the precondition for all NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) well planning. It is also the reason why seismic datasets, well logs, and the resource databases maintained by Sokkeldirektoratet (the Norwegian Offshore Directorate) are organised around basin and play boundaries that ultimately trace back to plate-tectonic events (sokkeldirektoratet-factpages, Fields and discoveries).
