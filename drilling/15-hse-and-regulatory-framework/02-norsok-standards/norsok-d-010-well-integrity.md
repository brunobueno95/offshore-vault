## Overview

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard, titled "Well integrity in drilling and well operations") is the single most important technical standard governing how wells are drilled, completed, operated, intervened on, and permanently abandoned on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf). It is issued by Standards Norway and serves as the Norwegian petroleum sector's common reference for well integrity across every operator, every drilling contractor, and every service company working on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf). When a Norwegian well programme, rig-specific well control manual, or kick-response drill refers to "the standard," it is almost always referring to NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 1).

The standard exists because wells on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) have a long service life and an unforgiving environment. A well on Ekofisk may be drilled in the 1970s and still produce in the 2030s, passing through drilling, completion, production, intervention, and eventual P&A (Plug and Abandonment) under multiple operators and different contractor crews. Every one of those phases must preserve the same core property: containment of formation fluids inside the wellbore, where they belong, for the full life of the well. NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) codifies the two-barrier philosophy, the inventory of tested components that count as barriers, and the verification regime that keeps those barriers trustworthy through every life-cycle phase (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6; iso-16530-1, Life-cycle model).

The current revision is Rev 5, issued in 2021 (norsok-d-010-rev5, Scope and applicability). It is a paywalled document and must be purchased from Standards Norway, but its requirements are effectively public knowledge because they are reflected in every operator well programme, every rig-specific well control manual, and every IWCF (International Well Control Forum) and IADC (International Association of Drilling Contractors) training syllabus taught in Norway (iwcf-level-2, Core content; iadc-drilling-manual, Well integrity chapter). This article explains what NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) requires, how it relates to Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority, formerly Petroleumstilsynet) regulation, how it is applied in practice at the rig, and where it sits in the broader global standards landscape.

## Details

### Origin and purpose

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) was first issued in 1996 as part of the NORSOK programme, a Norwegian petroleum sector initiative to develop common standards that improved cost competitiveness of NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) operations without reducing safety. Before NORSOK, each operator maintained its own well-integrity company standards, and each rig contractor maintained its own shut-in and testing procedures. The result was duplicated engineering, inconsistent expectations between operators, and a fragmented training pipeline. NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) replaced that patchwork with a single technical reference that every operator and every rig contractor on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) uses (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 1).

The standard's stated purpose is to define minimum functional and performance requirements for well integrity throughout the life cycle of a well. It does not prescribe specific equipment models or specific step-by-step procedures. Instead it specifies functional requirements that any compliant equipment or procedure must meet. This functional orientation is what lets NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) apply equally to a surface BOP (Blowout Preventer) fixed-platform well on Ekofisk, a subsea BOP (Blowout Preventer) floating-rig exploration well in the Barents Sea, and a wireline intervention on a Troll subsea production well (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 5).

### The two-barrier philosophy

The core principle of NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) is the two-barrier philosophy, called the tobarriereprinsippet (two-barrier principle) in Norwegian. At all times during well construction and operation, there must be two independent, tested, and verifiable barriers between formation fluids under pressure and the external environment. One is the primary barrier, the one that acts first. One is the secondary barrier, the one that acts if the primary fails. The two must be independent: a single common failure must not compromise both (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6; api-rp-59, Barrier philosophy).

During normal drilling, the primary barrier is the drilling mud column, which holds back formation fluids by hydrostatic pressure. The secondary barrier is the BOP (Blowout Preventer) stack combined with the casing string, the cemented casing-hanger seal assembly, and the wellhead. If the mud column fails, for example by taking a kick (an unwanted influx of formation fluid into the wellbore under pressure) that unloads the column, the BOP (Blowout Preventer) closes on the drill string and becomes the credited barrier that holds back the flow until the well is killed (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6; iwcf-level-2, Two-barrier module).

During a production phase, the primary barrier is typically the completion string combined with the production packer (a seal assembly that isolates the producing interval's annulus from the upper completion). The secondary barrier is typically the production casing, the production-casing cement column, the tubing hanger seal assembly, and the christmas tree valves (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6).

The philosophy is simple in principle and exacting in practice. Every credited barrier must be a specific, named, physically tested component. Every component must have a documented verification method on a defined interval. Every barrier transition, for example when the BOP (Blowout Preventer) is replaced by the christmas tree during completion hand-over, must be planned so that two barriers exist continuously through the transition, with no interval where only one is in place (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6; offshore-norge-117, Integrity across life cycle).

### Well Barrier Element (WBE) and Well Barrier Schematic (WBS)

A WBE (Well Barrier Element), called a brønnbarriereelement (well barrier element) in Norwegian, is any individual physical component that contributes to a barrier. Examples include a casing string cemented to a specified top-of-cement depth, a tested casing shoe track with a float collar and float shoe, a production packer set at a named depth, an annular preventer (a BOP (Blowout Preventer) element that uses a rubber packing element to seal around any object in the bore) installed in a given BOP (Blowout Preventer) stack, and a cement P&A (Plug and Abandonment) plug of a specified length set across a specified formation (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 4; Clause 6).

A WBS (Well Barrier Schematic), called a brønnbarriereskjema (well barrier schematic) in Norwegian, is a diagram showing every WBE (Well Barrier Element) in a well and how those elements combine to form the primary and secondary barriers for each life-cycle phase. Every operator on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) maintains a WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) for every well they operate, and the WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) is updated every time the barrier composition changes, for example when a casing string is run, when a cement plug is set, or when a completion is pulled (norsok-d-010-rev5, Annex A; offshore-norge-117, Integrity across life cycle).

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) Annex A provides illustrative WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) examples for typical well construction, completion, intervention, and P&A (Plug and Abandonment) phases. The annex is widely adopted as the reference format on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf): operator WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) documents visually follow the annex convention, with primary-barrier elements drawn in blue and secondary-barrier elements drawn in red (norsok-d-010-rev5, Annex A).

### Life-cycle coverage

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) covers every phase of a well's life from spud to permanent abandonment. The main phases addressed are:

1. **Well construction.** Drilling, casing, and cementing operations. Primary barrier is the mud column; secondary barrier is the BOP (Blowout Preventer) stack together with the casing and wellhead assembly (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6).
2. **Completion.** Installation of the production tubing, the production packer, the subsurface safety valve, the tubing hanger, and the christmas tree. Two-barrier continuity must be maintained across the BOP (Blowout Preventer) to christmas tree transition (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6).
3. **Production and injection.** Continuous integrity monitoring, annulus pressure surveillance, periodic function-testing of the subsurface safety valve, and periodic barrier re-verification (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7; offshore-norge-117, Integrity across life cycle).
4. **Well intervention.** Wireline, coiled tubing, and snubbing operations in which the christmas tree is replaced or supplemented by intervention-specific barrier equipment such as a lubricator and its BOP (Blowout Preventer) (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6).
5. **Suspension and P&A (Plug and Abandonment).** Temporary suspension barriers, permanent P&A (Plug and Abandonment) plugs, primary and secondary plug pairing, and the transition to the decommissioning phase (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 9).

Each phase has its own barrier expectations, testing intervals, and documentation requirements. The standard is structured so that a WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) for any phase can be assembled from its building blocks, and the barrier handover between phases is explicitly planned and documented (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 6; Clause 9; iso-16530-1, Life-cycle model).

### Testing and verification

A barrier counts only if it is tested. NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) defines the test method, test pressure, and test interval for every category of WBE (Well Barrier Element). Representative examples include:

- **BOP (Blowout Preventer) pressure tests.** Full pressure tests are applied on specified intervals, typically 14 days for surface BOP (Blowout Preventer) stacks and 21 days for subsea BOP (Blowout Preventer) stacks on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf), with defined test pressures keyed to the stack's rated working pressure (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7; api-std-53, Section 7).
- **BOP (Blowout Preventer) function tests.** Function tests are performed on shorter intervals, typically weekly, confirming ram closure, annular closure, and accumulator response (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7; api-std-53, Section 6).
- **Casing pressure tests.** Each casing string is pressure-tested after cementing to a specified test pressure above the expected maximum operating pressure, confirming integrity of the casing and the cement seal (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7).
- **FIT (Formation Integrity Test) or LOT (Leak-Off Test).** Applied after drilling out the casing shoe to establish the formation's fracture margin. The resulting value feeds into the MAASP (Maximum Allowable Annular Surface Pressure) calculation used in kill planning and in subsequent hole-section design (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7).
- **Cement barrier verification.** Pressure tests, weight tests, or tagging tests applied to cement P&A (Plug and Abandonment) plugs to confirm they form credited barriers (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 9; api-rp-10b, Cement barrier testing).
- **Annulus pressure surveillance** on production wells, confirming that sustained casing pressure has not developed above the threshold that indicates a barrier leak (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 7; offshore-norge-117, Integrity across life cycle).

The testing regime is not optional. A component that has not passed its scheduled test within the required interval does not count as a barrier, and operations that depend on it cannot proceed. This is the specific mechanism by which NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) makes the two-barrier philosophy operational rather than merely aspirational (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 5; Clause 7).

### Relationship to API, ISO, and IWCF standards

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) does not attempt to replace international equipment standards. Instead it references them. BOP (Blowout Preventer) equipment is specified to API (American Petroleum Institute) Spec 16A for drill-through equipment and API (American Petroleum Institute) Spec 16D for control systems for drilling-through equipment. BOP (Blowout Preventer) system performance requirements and testing follow API (American Petroleum Institute) STD 53. Cement testing and cement-plug verification follow API (American Petroleum Institute) RP 10B. Well control operations follow API (American Petroleum Institute) RP 59 as a companion reference that complements the NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) requirements without replacing them (api-std-53, General; api-rp-59, Scope; api-rp-10b, Cement barrier testing).

At the international well-integrity level, ISO 16530-1 (the International Organization for Standardization well integrity life-cycle standard, first published 2017) codifies many of the same principles for the global industry. NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) and ISO 16530-1 (the International Organization for Standardization well integrity life-cycle standard) are largely compatible. An operator that complies with NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) generally also complies with ISO 16530-1 (the International Organization for Standardization well integrity life-cycle standard), though some NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) requirements, especially on P&A (Plug and Abandonment) plug geometry and verification, go further than ISO (International Organization for Standardization) requires (iso-16530-1, Scope; iso-16530-1, Life-cycle model).

On the competence side, IWCF (International Well Control Forum) Level 2, Level 3, and Level 4 syllabi are the training qualifications recognised on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) for drillers, toolpushers, and drilling supervisors respectively. IWCF (International Well Control Forum) training incorporates the NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) two-barrier philosophy as part of its core content for any candidate drilling or supervising on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf). IADC (International Association of Drilling Contractors) WellCAP certification, common on North American rigs, is accepted on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) only with supplementary NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) orientation that covers NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) specifically (iwcf-level-2, Core content; iadc-drilling-manual, Well control chapter).

### Revisions

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) has been revised several times since its original 1996 edition. Rev 1 was issued in 1996, Rev 2 in 2000, Rev 3 in 2004 as a substantial reorganisation that introduced the now-standard WBS (Well Barrier Schematic) format in Annex A, Rev 4 in 2013, and Rev 5 in 2021 as the current revision (norsok-d-010-rev5, Scope and applicability).

Revisions typically consolidate interpretive clarifications that operators, drilling contractors, and Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority) have agreed during the previous revision cycle. An operator well programme specifies which revision applies to a given well. Many producing wells on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) were drilled under an earlier revision and are maintained under the revision that applied at the time of construction, with integrity re-assessments performed under the current revision when major interventions or P&A (Plug and Abandonment) are planned (norsok-d-010-rev5, Scope and applicability; offshore-norge-117, Integrity across life cycle).

## NCS-specific context

NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) has no formal legal force on its own. Its authority on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) comes from how it is referenced by Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority) in the Innretningsforskriften (the Facilities Regulations) and the Aktivitetsforskriften (the Activities Regulations). Those two regulations, together with the Styringsforskriften (the Management Regulations), require that facilities and activities comply with recognised standards for well integrity and for the management of risk. The Innretningsforskriften (the Facilities Regulations) guidance explicitly names NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) as an accepted recognised standard for well barriers, and the Aktivitetsforskriften (the Activities Regulations) guidance does the same for operational activities. An operator that follows NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) is therefore in a known compliance position by default. An operator that deviates must justify the deviation and demonstrate equivalent integrity by another route acceptable to Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority) (havtil-innretningsforskriften, Section 48 on well barriers; havtil-aktivitetsforskriften, Section 85 on well control; havtil-styringsforskriften, Recognised standards).

Offshore Norge Guideline 117 (the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) industry guideline on well integrity) is the operator-level companion to NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard). Guideline 117 codifies cross-operator practice for well integrity management across the producing-well population on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) and is widely adopted by Equinor, Aker BP, Vår Energi, ConocoPhillips Skandinavia, and the other active NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) operators. Where NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) defines the standard itself, Guideline 117 explains how NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) operators implement it operationally across portfolios of hundreds of wells (offshore-norge-117, Purpose; offshore-norge-117, Roles and responsibilities).

At the rig, enforcement of NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) sits with the boresjef (toolpusher), sometimes also titled the borebas (toolpusher) on older installations, supported by the boreleder (drilling supervisor). The boresjef (toolpusher) is rig-side responsible for ensuring that every BOP (Blowout Preventer) pressure test, function test, and barrier verification required by NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) is executed on its interval and documented in the rig's well-control logs. The boreleder (drilling supervisor), as the operator's senior onsite representative, is operator-side responsible for confirming barrier compliance against the well programme and for countersigning the BOP (Blowout Preventer) and barrier test records before the rig moves from one hole section to the next. Failure to maintain a credited second barrier stops drilling until the condition is restored (norsok-d-010-rev5, Clause 5; offshore-norge-117, Roles and responsibilities).

Incident reporting related to barrier failure feeds into Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority) notification requirements and into the annual RNNP (Risk level in Norwegian petroleum activities, the Norwegian sector-wide risk indicator compiled by Havtil (Havindustritilsynet, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority)) report. Well control incidents classified under Offshore Norge Guideline 135 (the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) guideline on well control incident classification and reporting) are tagged against the specific NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) requirement that was challenged during the event. This link between standard requirement and sector-level incident statistics is what keeps NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) continuously evolving rather than static between revisions (offshore-norge-135, Classification; offshore-norge-135, Notification).

NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) operators have used NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) as the reference for high-profile incident investigations. The Snorre A gas blowout of 2004, investigated by what was then Petroleumstilsynet (now Havtil, the Norwegian Ocean Industry Authority), was analysed against the then-current revision's two-barrier philosophy. The Gullfaks C well control incident of 2010 was likewise framed by the NORSOK D-010 (the Norwegian well integrity standard) requirement that a second barrier be continuously credited during well construction. Both investigations reinforced specific clauses in subsequent revisions, and both are standard teaching material in IWCF (International Well Control Forum) Level 3 and Level 4 training on the NCS (Norwegian Continental Shelf) (iwcf-level-2, Core content).
